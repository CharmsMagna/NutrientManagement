What is the Link Between Water Use and Nutrient Load on the Environment?

Water use and nutrient load are intrinsically connected through wastewater treatment and discharge. Every drop of water used in homes, businesses, and industries ultimately carries nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus into the environment. These nutrients, originating from detergents, fertilizers, and organic waste, enter watercourses through wastewater treatment plant discharges or surface runoff, contributing to eutrophication and poor water quality in rivers, lakes, and estuaries. Managing water use effectively reduces the volume of wastewater requiring treatment, thereby limiting nutrient loads and alleviating pressure on aquatic ecosystems.

What are the Nutrient Neutrality Drivers?

  • Habitats Regulations Assessment
  • Nutrient Neutrality Guidance (Natural England)

Nutrient neutrality is a key requirement under the Habitats Regulations Assessment (HRA) framework, ensuring that new developments do not increase nutrient pollution in protected sites such as Special Areas of Conservation (SACs) and Special Protection Areas (SPAs). The Natural England Nutrient Neutrality Guidance provides a framework for developers, local authorities, and water companies to offset nutrient loads by implementing mitigation measures. This includes wetland creation, land use change, and advanced wastewater treatment technologies. The objective is to prevent further ecological damage while enabling sustainable development.

What is Water Saving in Perpetuity?

  • Retrofitting existing properties with water-efficient fixtures
  • Mandating water efficiency in new developments
  • Encouraging behaviour change through education and incentives
  • Ensuring long-term monitoring and reporting on water-saving measures

By securing water savings indefinitely, we can help mitigate nutrient pollution, support biodiversity, and ensure water resilience in a changing climate.

What is Temporary Water Saving?

Temporary water saving is to alleviate short-term abstraction pressure & wastewater treatment plant upgrades. Reducing water consumption directly alleviates pressure on both water resources and wastewater infrastructure.

  • Water efficiency campaigns targeting high-consumption areas
  • Temporary water restrictions during drought periods
  • Accelerated investment in wastewater treatment plant upgrades
  • Deployment of smart water metering and leakage reduction initiatives
